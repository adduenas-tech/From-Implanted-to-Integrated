The Problem

When a district contracts with an ABA agency, the typical model places a BCBA supervising an RBT in a 1:1 relationship with one student. The BCBA's focus stays within that dyad ‚Äî while the classroom, teaching team, and school ecosystem remain largely untouched. Research calls this an "additive" or siloed model. The result: interventions that work in the bubble but don't generalize, and school staff who feel excluded rather than empowered.

1
Shift from Dyad Supervision to Classroom Ecology
Step outside the RBT‚Äìstudent bubble
‚ñæ

Step outside the BCBA-RBT-student bubble and observe the whole classroom environment. Look at antecedents in the physical space, teacher interactions, peer dynamics, and routine structures.

  • Conduct regular ecological observations ‚Äî not just data checks on the student
  • Identify environmental variables (seating, transitions, noise, pacing) that affect behavior
  • Share findings with the full classroom team, not just the RBT
  • Use your data to inform classroom-wide adjustments, not just individual plans
üìÑ Reilly & Thoele (2025) ¬∑ Behavior Analysis in Practice
2
Provide Professional Development, Not Just Behavior Plans
Position yourself as an instructional partner
‚ñæ

Teachers implement strategies daily. If they don't understand the "why" behind a plan, fidelity drops. Position yourself as a capacity-builder, not just a plan-writer.

  • Offer brief, practical in-service training on behavioral principles in plain language
  • Avoid ABA jargon ‚Äî research shows technical language reduces teacher buy-in
  • Co-develop strategies that fit naturally into existing classroom routines
  • Acknowledge what teachers already do well and build from there
üìÑ PMC (2022) ¬∑ BCBA School Fidelity Study
3
Use an Intentional Fading Model
Transfer knowledge into the classroom
‚ñæ

The goal should be to transfer knowledge into the classroom ecosystem ‚Äî not to remain the permanent expert. Plan from the start for how your role will fade as teacher capacity grows.

  • Begin by modeling interventions directly with the student alongside the teacher
  • Gradually shift implementation to the teacher while you observe and coach
  • Create clear milestones for when teacher-led implementation is the primary approach
  • Build independence in the school, not reliance on the agency
üìÑ Traub et al. (2017) ¬∑ Fading Support Models
4
Assess Social Validity ‚Äî Does the Plan Fit the Classroom?
Co-create with contextual fit in mind
‚ñæ

A behavior plan that works in theory but is unworkable for a teacher with 20 other students will never be implemented with fidelity. Co-create plans with contextual fit from the start.

  • Ask teachers directly: "Is this feasible in your day?"
  • Adapt goals and procedures to match classroom rhythms and structures
  • Revisit plans regularly with the whole team ‚Äî not just the RBT
  • Treat teacher feedback as data, not resistance
üìÑ PMC (2025) ¬∑ BCBA‚ÄìTeacher Collaboration Factors
5
Learn the Language of Education
Become fluent in the school's systems
‚ñæ

BCBAs often enter schools without training in educational law, IEP systems, PBIS tiers, or school culture. Fluency in these systems is essential to becoming a true ecosystem partner.

  • Study IDEA, FAPE, LRE, and other legal frameworks governing your student's placement
  • Participate in IEP meetings as a collaborative team member, not just a service provider
  • Engage with PBIS teams and Tier 1/2 supports ‚Äî not just Tier 3 crisis intervention
  • Learn each school's culture, norms, and staff dynamics before proposing changes
üìÑ Layden et al. (2024) ¬∑ Roles & Responsibilities Survey

Crossing Disciplinary Boundaries

Not all teamwork looks the same. The terms multidisciplinary, interdisciplinary, and transdisciplinary describe meaningfully different levels of professional integration ‚Äî and choosing the right approach shapes student outcomes. Explore the interactive table and model spotlights below to understand the key distinctions.

How professionals work Independently, in parallel; each stays within their own disciplinary lane Together but with identifiable disciplinary roles; goals are shared Roles blur intentionally; knowledge and skills are actively shared across disciplines
üí° Think of it this way: In a multidisciplinary team, the SLP writes their goals, the OT writes theirs, and the special educator writes theirs ‚Äî all for the same student, but independently. In an interdisciplinary team they meet and coordinate. In a transdisciplinary team, the SLP teaches the teacher how to embed communication strategies during math, and the BCBA teaches the SLP how to collect data with precision ‚Äî roles expand and release. (Bowman et al., 2021; Reilly et al., 2025)
Communication frequency Minimal; mainly to coordinate or report ‚Äî ongoing communication not essential More frequent; team meetings to develop shared goals and review progress Continuous and deliberate; uses common jargon-free vocabulary across all disciplines
üí° In multidisciplinary models, communication failures are common because updates aren't structurally required. Research shows that poor communication ‚Äî not just skill gaps ‚Äî is linked directly to poor student outcomes and even patient safety events. Transdisciplinary teams address this with scheduled team rounds, shared data systems, and email groups. (Bowman et al., 2021; Castro-Kemp et al., 2021)
Goal development Each professional sets goals independently for their domain Professionals develop goals cooperatively after independent assessments Unified, holistic goals co-created by the full team including family; no domain silos
üí° Transdisciplinary goal-setting answers: What should we change? How should we change it? How will we know it worked? ‚Äî and all disciplines answer together. This is especially critical for students with ASD or IDD whose social, communication, behavioral, and academic needs are deeply intertwined. (Reilly et al., 2025; Bowman et al., 2021)
Professional roles Strictly maintained; each professional operates within their own boundaries Roles are preserved but perspectives broaden beyond disciplinary limits Role release + role expansion: professionals teach their skills to teammates and learn from them
üí° Role release means deliberately sharing your expertise with others so they can implement it (e.g., a BCBA teaches a teacher to use a token economy). Role expansion means learning new skills beyond your traditional scope (e.g., a BCBA learning how PBIS tiers map to educational law). This is the defining feature of transdisciplinary practice. (Bowman et al., 2021; Reilly et al., 2025)
Integration of expertise Additive ‚Äî contributions sit side by side but don't influence each other Coordinated ‚Äî expertise is combined to produce shared plans Synergistic ‚Äî a unified framework emerges that couldn't exist from any one discipline alone
üí° The analogy used in the literature is vivid: multidisciplinary = a salad (ingredients distinct and separate), interdisciplinary = a soup (ingredients blend but remain identifiable), transdisciplinary = a cake (ingredients fully combined into something new). (Choi & Pak, 2006, as cited in Bowman et al., 2021)
Decision-making Each professional makes autonomous decisions within their area Joint decisions on goals; independent implementation within disciplines Collective ownership ‚Äî all decisions, data, programs, and outcomes are shared
üí° Transdisciplinary collective ownership means if an intervention isn't working, the whole team owns that ‚Äî not just the BCBA or the teacher. This promotes accountability without blame, and research links it to reduced professional conflict and burnout. (Bowman et al., 2021; Strogilos et al., 2011)
Best suited for Situations requiring diverse expert input without deep integration Students with mild-to-moderate disabilities in inclusive settings with moderate complexity Students with complex, intersecting needs (ASD, IDD, severe disabilities) requiring whole-team integration
üí° Evidence shows integrated service models (closest to transdisciplinary) are superior for students with profound/multiple learning difficulties, while classroom-based collaborative approaches are most effective for mild-moderate disabilities in inclusive settings. Context matters: the "best" model depends on population, resources, and organizational support. (Selanikyo et al., 2017; Strogilos et al., 2011; Castro-Kemp et al., 2021)
Common barriers Eclecticism risk; siloed practice; no shared accountability Professionals work together to plan but may revert to silos during implementation Requires substantial time, shared management, training, and trust to implement well
üí° Multidisciplinary models risk "eclecticism" ‚Äî a haphazard mix of interventions with no coherent evidence base. Research warns that multidisciplinary practice without effective collaboration may actually be counterproductive. Transdisciplinary models face real structural barriers (time, organizational support, funding), but these are addressable with intentional planning. (Bowman et al., 2021; Salminen et al., 2024)

Multidisciplinary
Analogy: The Salad ‚Äî ingredients distinct & separate

In a multidisciplinary model, professionals work in parallel ‚Äî each operating within their own discipline's boundaries. A BCBA writes a behavior plan, the SLP writes communication goals, the OT writes sensory goals. These are additive, not integrative. Information may be exchanged to coordinate care, but frequent ongoing communication isn't seen as essential because each professional's plan doesn't fundamentally change based on what another discipline does.

While this offers more perspectives than a single-discipline approach, the narrow discipline-specific viewpoint can inhibit a holistic understanding of complex students. Without a shared framework, this model risks sliding into "eclecticism" ‚Äî a mix of interventions that may be individually evidence-based but incoherent as a package.

Parallel practice Additive contributions Discipline-specific goals Minimal communication Siloed accountability
üç≤
Interdisciplinary
Analogy: The Soup ‚Äî ingredients blend but remain identifiable

Interdisciplinary teams take a more comprehensive and integrative approach. Professionals conduct their own assessments independently, then come together to create shared goals and coordinate implementation. Distinct disciplinary roles are preserved while responsibilities and outcomes are shared.

This model requires more frequent interaction, and team members often expand their clinical perspectives beyond their own discipline's boundaries. Think of the BCBA and teacher meeting weekly to align on a student's goals ‚Äî each still brings their own expertise to the table, but they're steering in the same direction. This is a significant upgrade from multidisciplinary, though true collaboration isn't yet achieved because professionals still implement independently.

Shared goals Coordinated roles Regular team meetings Joint goal-setting Independent implementation
üéÇ
Transdisciplinary
Analogy: The Cake ‚Äî ingredients fully merged into something new

Transdisciplinary collaboration synthesizes the expertise of every team member by intentionally blurring disciplinary boundaries. This means two defining features: role release (sharing your specialized knowledge so others can implement it) and role expansion (learning skills beyond your traditional scope). A BCBA might teach a classroom teacher to use a token economy with fidelity; a speech-language pathologist might learn to take precise behavioral data.

Knowledge is deliberately shared through frequent communication using a common, jargon-free vocabulary. This model demands mutual trust, respect, and a shared unity of purpose. The resulting product is greater than the sum of its individual parts ‚Äî leading to holistic interventions that address the complex, intertwined needs of students with ASD and other developmental disabilities. Research consistently finds this approach most effective when structural supports (shared management, allocated planning time, coordination roles) are in place.

Role release Role expansion Unified framework Shared ownership Jargon-free language Best for complex needs
